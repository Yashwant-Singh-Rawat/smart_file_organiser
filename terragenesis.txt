That is a sharp piece of lore analysis. You've hit on the Geopolitical Reality that most players ignore because it’s not a button you can click in the UI.
In the TerraGenesis universe, Horizon Corporation operates as a post-national entity. While the FFI is a think-tank that has to actually do the science, and the Sons of Hephaestus are a decentralized cult of purists, Horizon is the only faction that treats "The Future" as a commodity.

The "Buy-to-Build" Advantage
You’re right that only the UNSA and Gaia could even attempt to play on that field, but for different reasons:
* Horizon (The Market): They don't need to invent the Magnetic Shield Generator; they just need to find the lead researcher at the FFI who has a gambling debt or a desire for a private moon base. Their "lore" advantage is liquidity. In a galaxy where resources are scarce, a corporation that controls the flow of Credits controls the patents.
* UNSA (The Treaty): They compete through Bureaucracy. They don't "buy" tech; they "requisition" it in the name of "Global Earth Interests." If Horizon buys a patent, the UNSA tries to pass a resolution making it public domain for the "good of the refugees."
* Daughters of Gaia (The Grassroots): Their competition is Ideological. They have a massive civilian following. They don't buy tech; they get it through "donations" from sympathetic scientists who believe in the Green Cause.

The FFI's Vulnerability
This puts your chosen faction, the Far-Future Institute, in a precarious spot. In the lore, the FFI is the "Laboratory of the Galaxy."
* The Problem: You have the best brains, but you lack the Security of the UNSA and the Treasury of Horizon.
* The Risk: Every time you research a "Level X" facility, Horizon is likely hovering nearby with a checkbook (or a corporate espionage team) ready to "acquire" your hard work.
The "Sons of Hephaestus" Exception
The only faction that can't compete in this tech-trading meta is the Sons of Hephaestus. They aren't just broke; they're Luddites (by choice). They wouldn't buy a terraforming patent if Horizon offered it for a penny—they’d probably try to delete the data to "save" the planet from being changed.
